WPC 150 Case Segment

The Story of the Bean: Agribusiness Lens

Begin the case by following a coffee bean before it becomes a menu item, a price, or a brand promise.

Written Case Narrative

Read the opening and Agribusiness segment, then move to the practice tab when you are ready to apply the case evidence.

Case Premise

Every cup of coffee begins before the coffee shop. It begins with land, weather, labor, farming knowledge, processing decisions, buyer relationships, financing, price signals, roasting choices, store operations, and customer trust. By the time a student buys a drink before class, the bean has already moved through a business system where people made decisions under uncertainty.

This case follows Copper Cup Coffee, a fictional Arizona coffee company with one busy location near campus. Copper Cup has grown by selling approachable drinks at student-friendly prices. Its founder, Elena Torres, wants the company to launch a featured roast called The Story of the Bean. The roast would let customers trace a coffee bean from a farmer cooperative to the cafe counter.

Elena is not trying to make a marketing slogan. She is trying to answer a business question:

Should Copper Cup pay more for a transparent, resilient, farmer-connected coffee supply, even if doing so creates higher costs, operational pressure, and pricing challenges?

AI at Work: Framing the Problem

Elena could use AI to summarize customer questions, draft possible launch messages, or compare the three sourcing options in plain language. The risk is that AI may produce language that sounds confident before Copper Cup has evidence. Any AI-assisted summary still needs human review for accuracy, missing stakeholders, and unsupported claims.

Students will revisit that same question through W. P. Carey disciplines. This first segment begins with Agribusiness because the bean begins as an agricultural product. The other disciplines do not replace that origin story. They add lenses that help Copper Cup understand what happens when the bean becomes a price, an investment, and a managed organization.

The Decision That Connects the Case

Copper Cup is considering three sourcing paths for The Story of the Bean.

Market purchase: Copper Cup buys green coffee through a broker at the current market price. This keeps purchasing flexible and may support lower menu prices, but the company has limited visibility into farmer income, environmental practices, and working conditions.

Certified sustainability purchase: Copper Cup pays a moderate premium for coffee carrying a recognized sustainability certification. This gives customers a visible signal and gives the company a clearer responsibility claim, but certification may not answer every question about value distribution.

Direct cooperative partnership: Copper Cup signs a longer-term agreement with a farmer cooperative, pays a higher price, helps fund basic traceability, and tells customers more about the bean's journey. This may create stronger relationships and learning, but it creates higher cost, supply risk, and management complexity.

No option is perfect. That is the point of the case. A business decision becomes meaningful when students can see what each option makes possible, what each option makes difficult, and who bears the consequences.

AI at Work: Comparing Options

Maya could ask AI to create a decision table that compares cost, transparency, risk, stakeholder impact, and evidence needed for each sourcing path. That table would be a starting point, not the decision itself. Students should ask what assumptions the table makes, what data it lacks, and whether it treats certification, traceability, and fairness as the same thing.

Part 1: Agribusiness - Before the Bean Has a Brand

Maya Alvarez is a first-year W. P. Carey student who works part time at Copper Cup. Elena asks her to help prepare a short launch display for The Story of the Bean. Maya starts with what seems like a simple question:

"How does this coffee bean get from a farm to our shop?"

The answer changes the whole project.

The cooperative explains that coffee is not just grown and shipped. Coffee cherries are planted, tended, harvested, processed, dried, sorted, graded, bagged, exported, imported, roasted, packaged, brewed, and sold. Each stage depends on decisions made by different people with different incentives and constraints. Farmers manage land, labor, weather, pests, soil, water, and family income. Processors protect quality and reduce waste. Buyers evaluate price, flavor, risk, certification, and reliability. Roasters transform green coffee into a product customers recognize. The cafe turns the bean into an experience people are willing to pay for.

Maya learns that coffee supports millions of farmers globally and creates work across the value chain, but the people closest to production may still face unstable income, climate pressure, and limited bargaining power. That tension fits the Agribusiness lens from WPC 150: food and agricultural products move through connected systems where production, markets, policy, sustainability, technology, and communities interact.

Copper Cup's potential partner is a smallholder cooperative. It can sell coffee to many buyers, but long-term planning is hard. Weather has become less predictable. Some farmers need better drying equipment so rain does not damage quality after harvest. The cooperative wants more stable buyer relationships because market prices can swing while farm costs remain very real. Younger family members are beginning to question whether coffee farming can support a future.

Elena sees the appeal of the direct partnership. Copper Cup could pay a higher price, learn more about the cooperative, and explain the relationship honestly to customers. But she also sees the limits. A small coffee shop cannot solve global coffee inequality by itself. A label, a premium, or a QR code is not the same as justice. Copper Cup needs to be precise about what it can actually claim.

AI at Work: Mapping the System

Maya could use AI to organize the farm-to-cup system into categories such as inputs, production, processing, movement, markets, and responsibility. AI can help structure complexity, but it cannot verify farmer income, labor conditions, climate effects, or cooperative needs unless Copper Cup supplies trustworthy evidence.

Maya maps the farm-to-cup system:

  • Inputs: land, water, seedlings, shade, fertilizer, labor, equipment, credit, knowledge, and data.
  • Production: growing coffee cherries, managing pests and climate stress, harvesting at the right time.
  • Processing: washing or drying, sorting, grading, storing, and preparing beans for export.
  • Movement: cooperative aggregation, exporter, importer, freight, roasting, packaging, and cafe delivery.
  • Market: commodity price, quality premiums, certifications, buyer contracts, customer demand, and menu price.
  • Responsibility: farmer income, environmental stewardship, labor conditions, climate resilience, food-system livelihoods, and truthful claims.

Agribusiness makes the first disciplinary contribution: it keeps Copper Cup from treating coffee as an input that simply appears on an invoice. The bean is part of a food and resource system. If Copper Cup claims to tell the story of the bean, the company has to ask which parts of the system are visible, which parts remain hidden, and whose work is being valued.

When the Bean Becomes a Food Product

Maya then realizes the next agribusiness question is not only where the bean came from. It is how the bean becomes a food product people can responsibly buy, sell, price, promote, and trust.

That shift brings in the Food Industry Management side of agribusiness. Copper Cup is not selling an undifferentiated coffee bean. It is trying to turn an agricultural product into a retail food experience. Maya has to think like a food industry manager: Who is the customer? What need does the product meet? What makes it different from every other coffee on the menu? How should the story appear in the cafe, on a mobile preorder screen, on a bag of roasted coffee, or in a customer receipt? What data would tell Copper Cup whether the launch is working?

Elena asks Maya to prepare the kind of launch brief a student team might bring to an industry mentor, buyer, trade show, or case competition. The brief must connect field-level evidence to food marketing, retail execution, channel strategy, data, and customer trust.

Artifact: Story of the Bean Launch Brief

Launch brief for The Story of the Bean
Product concept A featured roast and limited drink menu that trace coffee from a farmer cooperative to the cafe counter.
Target customer Students and campus neighbors who want quality coffee, a fair price, and a clearer story about where everyday food products come from.
Differentiation Origin relationship, cooperative premium, transparent evidence, flavor profile, and employee ability to explain what is known and unknown.
Channels In-store brewed coffee, small retail bags at the counter, and optional online preorder for pickup.
Customer communication Menu board note, receipt note, QR evidence page, employee talking points, and a short social post.
Claim boundary Avoid claims such as "fully ethical," "guaranteed fair," or "sustainable from farm to cup" unless Copper Cup can verify them.

Maya also sees how food retailing changes the decision. If Copper Cup sells only one featured latte, the story is limited to a premium drink. If it offers a small drip option, more students can try the coffee. If it sells retail bags, Copper Cup must manage inventory, packaging, shelf display, and online pickup. If it promotes the roast through mobile ordering, the story has to work without a barista there to explain it.

AI at Work: Reading Retail Signals

Maya could use AI to summarize customer questions, cluster QR-page comments, compare sales across channels, or draft a launch brief. But AI should not turn strong sales into a stronger responsibility claim. Sales data, customer curiosity, and evidence integrity have to be interpreted together.

The Food Industry Management lens is subtle but important here: agribusiness is not only production agriculture. It includes food marketing, food retailing, new product development, strategic pricing, supply networks, data, technology, risk, and applied industry problem solving.

Agribusiness Decision Point

Copper Cup must choose what kind of sourcing relationship it can responsibly defend.

Option A: Market purchase

  • Business upside: lower cost, purchasing flexibility, fewer management demands.
  • Business risk: limited transparency and weaker ability to support a farm-to-cup claim.
  • Social question: Does affordability for Copper Cup's customers justify less visibility into farmer outcomes?

Option B: Certified sustainability purchase

  • Business upside: recognizable signal, moderate premium, clearer communication.
  • Business risk: customers may assume the label solves more than it actually does.
  • Social question: What should a business verify before using a sustainability label as part of its story?

Option C: Direct cooperative partnership

  • Business upside: stronger relationship, richer learning, clearer value-chain story.
  • Business risk: higher cost, more responsibility, more exposure if the story is incomplete.
  • Social question: What does Copper Cup owe the people and places that make its product possible?

Agribusiness Case Prompt

Recommend one sourcing path. Explain how your recommendation affects the farm, the cooperative, Copper Cup, customers, and society. Then identify one claim Copper Cup should avoid making unless it has evidence.

AI at Work: Testing Stakeholder Perspectives

Students could use AI as a role-play partner by asking it to respond as a farmer cooperative, Copper Cup employee, skeptical customer, or cafe neighbor. The useful move is not accepting the role-play as truth. The useful move is using it to surface questions Copper Cup should investigate before making a final recommendation.

Agribusiness Takeaway

A strong agribusiness reading does not treat coffee as an input that simply appears on an invoice. It sees the bean as part of a food and resource system where farming, processing, logistics, markets, communities, sustainability, and truthful communication are connected.

The Food Industry Management lens adds the retail question: how does Copper Cup turn a responsible sourcing idea into a differentiated product, channel plan, price, data dashboard, and customer message?

AI can help organize the system, draft comparisons, and surface stakeholder questions, but Copper Cup still has to verify evidence before making claims about people, places, and sustainability.
